The Badger Divide

The Badger Divide

A 200 mile route from Inverness to Glasgow.

The Badger Divide is almost 200 miles of off road, traffic free cycling taking in man made paths, estate tracks and old roads. With only small portions of road to bring you in to Glasgow. The entire route can be ridden on a burly road bike such as a Surly Straggler or Mason Bokeh and tyres of 700 - 35c are recommended. The route can be ridden over two days with an early start for a challenging adventure or over three allowing you to take in some of the bothies on the route at an easier pace.

Starting in Inverness you follow the Great Glen Way down to Fort Augustus.  This section is by far the most strenuous but makes for some excellent riding. After leaving Fort Augustus you climb over the Corrieyairack pass and make your way along and over to Loch Rannoch via the Corrour estate main drag and Loch Ossian. This section passes by two bothies, a hostel and an excellent pub at Corrour Station which has rooms available via Airbnb. The bothies are right beside the route, one half way up the Corrieyairack and the second on the run down the other side. The hostel is SYHA Loch Ossian, it's pretty popular so booking well in advance is recommended.

If you plan on using these and if not already please consider joining the Mountain Bothy Association and supporting the amazing work that they do

You don't pass any shops during this part of the ride so stocking up at Fort Augustus is key if you don't think you'll make it to the pub in time. The next available refuel is either in Glen Lyon at the post office or Killin which is pretty far on an empty stomach.

Around half way the route passes by Kilvrecht Forestry Commission campsite and a covered picnic on the south side of Loch Rannoch, stopping here has worked out well whenever the route has previously been ridden over two days. Climbing away from here you head over to Glen Lyon where you pass the post office/cafe/general store. An enjoyable road section up the dead end Glen is a welcome break before arriving in Killin which has a decent sized store, cafes and a public toilet.

Following a mix of sustrans, forest paths and the Rob Roy Way the route heads toward Aberfoyle which also has a good selection of shops, cafes and pubs. The run in to Glasgow continues on through the Queen Elizabeth Forest then uses the start of the West Highland Way. There are a few gates towards the end of this which could be avoided by using the road if that suits better.

The final section that takes you to Kelvingrove Art Gallery uses the River Kelvin Path which is shared with pedestrians. SYHA Glasgow is less than a km from the finish and has an excellent bike room if you plan in staying the night in town.

The route lends itself to change pretty well and sections can be avoided and substituted with road or easier cycle path if required at various locations.

Using well established rights of way, heritage paths, estate roads and long trails such as The Great Glen Way. Without the hard work put in by organisation such as the Scottish rights of way Society this route would not be possible. The routes isn't endorsed or in collaboration with any of these organisations and please obey the outdoor access code if you ride it  http://www.outdooraccess-scotland.com If you meet any estate vehicles let them past freely as there probably doing something that pays for the maintenance of the various roads and tracks the route uses. See links below to find out more about these organisation that make this type of travel through Scotland possible: https://www.scotways.com http://www.highland.gov.uk/ggw http://www.heritagepaths.co.uk http://www.sustrans.org.uk/scotland/our-work-scotland http://scotland.forestry.gov.uk/forest-parks/tay-forest-park/kilvrecht-campsite http://scotland.forestry.gov.uk/forest-parks/queen-elizabeth-forest-park http://www.west-highland-way.co.uk
